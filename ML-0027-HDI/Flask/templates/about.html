Methodology

How the index — and this model — work.

The Human Development Index (HDI) was introduced to argue that a country's progress should be measured by what its people can do and become, not solely by the size of its economy. It combines three dimensions: a long and healthy life, access to knowledge, and a decent standard of living.

Inputs used by this model

The trained Linear Regression model takes four numeric indicators and outputs a continuous HDI score between 0 and 1:

HDI_Score ≈ β₁·Life_Expectancy + β₂·Mean_Years_Schooling
    + β₃·Expected_Years_Schooling + β₄·GNI_Per_Capita + intercept

The model was trained on a dataset of 167 countries, with missing values filled using column means and no categorical encoding required for the regression itself, since all four inputs are continuous.

Development tiers

Once a score is predicted, it is classified into one of four tiers:

TierScore RangeDescription
Very High 0.800 – 1.000 Long lifespans, near-universal education, high income levels.
High 0.700 – 0.799 Strong but uneven performance across the three dimensions.
Medium 0.550 – 0.699 Moderate outcomes; meaningful room for improvement in health or education.
Low 0.000 – 0.549 Significant development challenges across most indicators.

Limitations

This is a simplified, educational re-implementation of the HDI using Linear Regression rather than the UN Development Programme's official geometric-mean methodology. It is intended for learning purposes — to demonstrate an end-to-end ML workflow from raw data to a deployed prediction interface — and should not be used as an authoritative source on any country's actual HDI ranking.
